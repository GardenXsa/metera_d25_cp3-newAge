The Chronicles of Methera: A Tale of the Primordial Fabric
To understand the story of our world, you must first listen to the silent song that underpins all of creation. The ancients called it "Me'Te-Ra"—the Primordial Fabric, the Ethereal Weave. They knew, and rightly so, that the world was not sculpted from clay or carved from stone; it was woven from the Ether itself, like a great tapestry of fate. The Ether is not merely a force for spells. It is the blood that flows through Methera's veins, its breath, its dreams. It moves in unseen rivers, "Etheric Currents," that bring either life and growth, or chaos and mutation.
Sages who study the ancient texts say that this Weave is composed of different threads. There is the Anima Ether, the thread of life, which coaxes trees to reach for the sun and knits wounds closed; it is touched by druids and healers. There is the Forma Ether, the thread of change, from which illusionists spin their phantoms and the great mages of the past raised cities. There is the Ruina Ether, a dark thread of decay and entropy, dangerous and seductive, which necromancers reach for. There is the Nous Ether, the thread of thought, which allows seers to peer behind the veil of time and psions to move objects with their will. And, of course, there is the Elementa Ether, which manifests in the fury of fire, the depth of water, the stubbornness of earth, and the rage of the storm.
The boldest minds, those who have ventured into the ruins of Arcanum, whisper that Methera is not the only world. That it is but one island in a boundless Astral Sea, and that beyond its misty horizons lie other planes: The Umbra, a twisted and grim reflection of our own world; the Elemental Chaos, home to pure spirits of fire and wind; and the terrifying Abyss, a hypothetical reality of pure madness and decay. Perhaps before all of this, before the Ether itself, there existed even more ancient powers, the Silent Watchers, whose influence some cults still feel to this day.
The Age of Dawn: The Dragon's Slumber and the Awakening of the Younger Races
In the prehistoric twilight, when the world was young, the first beings to know the Ether were the dragons. They did not study magic—they were magic. As embodiments of elemental power, they reigned for millions of years, and the traces of their era can still be seen as scars upon the ancient mountains, still called the Dragon's Claw Ridge.
But the Ether is eternal motion, and under its influence, other peoples began to awaken. The Elves claim their ancestors gained consciousness by listening to the whispers of the stars through the forest leaves. The Dwarves believe they were forged by Methera herself in her stony heart. And humans, as always, appeared as if from nowhere—loud, industrious, and astonishingly adaptable.
The Golden Age: The Radiance of the Architects
And so came an era whose magnificence seems a mere fairy tale to the peoples of today. The age of those we call the Architects or the Forerunners. They called themselves the "Weavers of Reality." They were, most likely, an alliance of several highly advanced species who had achieved harmony with the Ether. They built cities of living crystal and frozen light, created self-learning automatons, and terraformed entire continents.
We remember the names of their cities from fragments of legends. Ilithia, the Sky-Citadel, which floated among the clouds until it fell into the western ocean, leaving behind only the scattered Floating Isles of Aethel—its weeping shards. Deep beneath the earth sprawled the metropolis of Xo'aan, where they studied the planet's heartbeat and where, perhaps, the Crystal Deeps were part of its power grid. They created Ether Engines, a Nexus Gate network between their cities, and mastered bio-engineering, spawning creatures that, having gone feral, have become the nightmares of the modern world.
But their greatness gave birth to a great pride. A schism grew within their society. The Aedificator faction sought to reach for the stars. The Custodian faction urged caution, fearing they were tearing the very fabric of reality. And the Transcender faction was the most dangerous of all. They sought ascension, a merging with the Ether itself, and conducted the most perilous experiments to achieve it.
The Great Sundering: The Day the World Was Wounded
What caused the catastrophe? Was it a desperate, out-of-control experiment by the Transcenders to tear open the veil between planes? Did their disagreements escalate into a war where reality itself was the weapon? Or did entities from other planes interfere in their affairs? Some philosophers believe it was simply an inevitable cycle of the Ether itself—a great, violent purge.
The consequences were cataclysmic. Uncontrolled Ether Storms swept across the planet. The land cracked open, creating Etheric Scars, the largest of which—the Bottomless Chasm—still bleeds raw magic to this day. The shining cities fell, their inhabitants slain or irrevocably mutated. Magic itself became wild and unpredictable, and the warped Ether gave birth to legions of monsters. The world fell into a silent horror.
The Age of Silence: Survival in the Darkness
The knowledge of the Architects was almost entirely lost. The peoples of the world were scattered and isolated. The dark ages of survival began. Humans devolved into scattered, barbaric tribes. The Elves retreated deep into the Whispering Forest, becoming reclusive and distrustful. The Dwarves sealed themselves within their mountain fortresses like Thunderhold, developing runic magic as a more stable and safe alternative to the chaos of the Ether. The places where the Architects' cities once stood became known as cursed lands.
The Age of Rebirth: A New Dawn and Old Shadows
But time passed. The Ether gradually calmed, and on the ruins of the old world, new civilizations were born.
Humans once again revealed their indomitable will. From an alliance of strong tribes, the mighty Aquilonian Empire was born. It is a centralized, militaristic state that values order and law. Its Legions are famed for their iron discipline, and its Imperial Magisterium controls the study of magic, forcing it into the framework of safe but rigid formulas. But behind the facade of order, the noble Houses constantly scheme. In addition to the Empire, other human cultures exist: a conglomerate of free trading cities on the southern coast, including Silver Haven; the ever-warring Kingdoms of the North; and the hardened nomads of the Ash Plains.
The Elves (Sylvanesti), the Children of the Forest, still prefer seclusion. They are divided into the High Elves, keepers of knowledge in hidden enclaves like the city of Elerion, and the wilder Wood Elves. Legends also whisper of the Dark Elves, renegades who turned to the darker aspects of the Ether, though their existence is unconfirmed. The Elves are wary of human expansionism and have a strained relationship with the dwarves due to the eternal conflict between nature and industry.
The Dwarves (Khazadrim), the Sons of Stone, have fortified their position, re-establishing trade and mining. Their society is divided into clans: the Stonefist Clan of Thunderhold is the most influential; the Deepdelver Clan are masters of crystalwork; and the Runesmith Clan are keepers of the art of runic magic, which can tame the Ether by binding its power into symbols.
The Orcs (Gronnar), the greenskins, are often seen as savages, but they possess their own tribal culture where strength and respect for the shamans who speak with spirits are paramount. Perhaps they were warped by the wild Ether, or perhaps they have always been this way—a harsh people from harsh lands.
The Beastkin (Kharash) is a collective term for a multitude of races with animalistic features. Their origin is a mystery—perhaps they are the result of Architect experiments, or a direct product of the Ether's influence. They live in tribes in the wilds or attempt to integrate into the melting pots of civilization, like Silver Haven.
Factions, Orders, and Secret Societies: The Great Game
In this reborn world, a constant struggle for power, knowledge, and survival is waged.
Military and state powers, like the Aquilonian Legions and the Imperial Magisterium, strive for order and the expansion of the Empire's influence.
Keepers of tradition, such as the Elven Circle of the Sylvan Wardens and the Dwarven Mining & Crafts Guild, protect the interests of their people.
Merchant and adventurer alliances. The Free Traders' Consortium is a shadow empire of merchants waging economic, and sometimes real, wars against Aquilonia. The Seekers of Lost Lore are a motley alliance of adventurers and scholars obsessed with the Forerunner ruins. They are not united: some seek knowledge, others fortune, and the most reckless among them seek personal power, risking a repeat of past mistakes.
Knightly and religious orders. The Order of the Crimson Flame is a fanatical order of knights dedicated to destroying etheric monsters, often with cruel and uncompromising methods. They are opposed by the Cult of the Sundered Sky, a dangerous sect that believes the Sundering was a blessing and seeks to bring about a new cataclysm.
Magic in Methera: A Gift and a Curse
Magic in the current age is multifaceted. There is Elementalism, the Nature Magic of the elves, the reliable Runecraft of the dwarves, deceptive Illusionism, the rare but potent Psionics, forbidden Necromancy, practical Alchemy, and the formalized Imperial Sorcery of Aquilonia. Power can be a birthright, a product of long study, or drawn from ancient Artifacts and Places of Power. But everything has its price: Etheric Exhaustion, a Backlash from a failed spell, the slow Corruption of body and mind from exposure to raw Ether, or attracting dangerous entities from other planes.
Living Legends and Quiet Tragedies: The Soul of the World
But the true history of Methera is not written only in the annals of emperors and archmages. It is written in the stories of those who live and die in this land of beauty and peril.
It is the story of General Kaelen Varrus, the "Iron Griffin" of Aquilonia. He is a hero of the Empire, a brilliant tactician who stands guard at the Etheric Chasm. But his heart has been scoured by grief: twenty years ago, the Chasm took his wife and daughter. Now his duty is an obsession. He will make any deal, take any risk, to find a weapon in the Architect ruins capable of closing that wound on the world's surface, not realizing that his desperation may birth a new cataclysm.
It is the story of the elf Lyraelle Silverleaf, a keeper of lore. In the ambitions of Aquilonia and the recklessness of the Seekers, she sees a terrifying echo of the Transcenders who doomed the world. She tries to warn others, but her people are too reclusive, and the other races dismiss her words as elven arrogance. Her tragedy is knowing the storm that is coming, and being powerless to stop it.
It is the story of the dwarf Borin Stonehand, an exile. Once a great runesmith, he was cast out for a ritual that fused his left arm with a living, pulsing etheric rune. Now this rune grants him incredible power but is slowly killing him, whispering maddening secrets from the Ether. He seeks the mythical Forge of the Ancestors not just for knowledge, but in a desperate hope of finding a cure.
But behind these great names lie quieter dramas. The drama of the elven botanist Elara, who returned after 150 years to the meadow where she said goodbye to her human friend, only to find a bustling city quarter in its place. And her entire adventure became a search for a single blossom from her past, pushing its way through a crack in a stone fountain—the last echo of a world that was no more.
Or the tale of the old dwarf blacksmith Gromli. A young adventurer brought him her great-great-grandfather's "heroic" shield to be repaired. But Gromli recognized his own apprentice work, forged 200 years ago for a cheerful sellsword who was no hero, but who died foolishly while saving Gromli's own life. Tormented by two centuries of guilt, he did not shatter her family legend. He silently reforged the shield, pouring all his accumulated mastery into it, transforming the symbol of his shame into a true masterpiece that would now genuinely protect her.
Or the legend of the orc shaman Groshnak, who stopped his young chief from performing a bloody ritual for glory by showing him that it was, in truth, a ritual of despair performed by their ancestors—a suicidal charge to buy time for their families to escape. He taught him that history is not only what you are proud of, but also what you mourn.
This is Methera. A world where the Empire's expansion clashes with elven isolationism, where border wars rage against orcs and monsters, where a secret race for the Architects' legacy is underway. A world where something ancient is stirring in the depths of the ruins. But it is also a world where heroes rise to challenge the darkness, and where even in the shadow of great events, there is room for quiet sorrow, redemption, and hope.
